Congress.

Everything you need to do — and everything you should do — to run for a seat in the Liberland Congress on the blockchain we use today.

The constitutional truth

The Constitution is the law of Liberland. Everything below is how it's currently implemented.

A Substrate chain, a Polkadot-style wallet, a Phragmén election, voted in LLM-weight.

The chain counts votes. It does not campaign for you. Become known, say what you mean.

§ Introduction

Why this guide exists

Liberland's Congress is elected by its citizens, on-chain, with stake-weighted votes. The blockchain handles the counting. Everything that gets you elected happens before that — and most of it is on you.

This primer walks through everything a serious Congressional candidate should do on the current blockchain — the Substrate-based chain operated at blockchain.liberland.org. Some items are mandatory: without them, the chain simply will not register your candidacy. Some are strongly encouraged: candidates who skip them tend to be invisible to most voters. Some are optional but differentiating: they're how a candidate becomes recognisable, trusted, and remembered.

Liberland is small. The active electorate is in the low thousands and most of them never meet each other in person. That means a single well-made video can reach a meaningful share of voters, and a single bad-faith move can follow you for years. Treat both seriously.

§ 1 — Prerequisites

Before anything else, the eligibility check

The current Liberland blockchain checks three things before it will accept a candidacy application. None of them are negotiable, and none can be waived.

RequirementWhat "fail" looks like
You are a full citizen with on-chain identity E-residents and applicants cannot run. You must have completed onboarding and claimed citizenship on-chain.
You hold at least 5,000 Merits (LLM) Below 5,000 LLM, you cannot apply. This threshold also gates voting and other political rights.
You are not on welfare Citizens currently receiving welfare benefits cannot run for Congress. This applies to voting eligibility as well.

The job is real work

The current docs are explicit: it takes a minimum of five hours a week to do the job of Liberland congressman properly. If you don't have those five hours every week for the next term, this is not the moment to run. Citizens read absence in Congress as bad faith.

§ 2 — The work, at a glance

Three tiers of candidate work

Not everything in this primer is equally important. Treat the three tiers as priorities, not a menu. The mandatory items must be done. The encouraged items are where serious candidates actually win or lose. The optional items separate good campaigns from memorable ones.

▼ Mandatory

On-chain registration

Without these, your candidacy literally does not exist in the system.

  • Citizenship onboarded on-chain
  • 5,000+ Merits in your account
  • Not on welfare
  • Candidacy submitted via the Congress tab
  • On-chain identity (display name) set
▼ Strongly encouraged

Becoming someone voters know

Skipping these means most citizens have no idea who you are on election day.

  • A short campaign video (1–3 min)
  • A candidate page (own or liberland.org)
  • An introduction post on the Forum
  • Presence in Liberland Chat (Matrix)
  • Clear position on 2–3 issues
▼ Optional

Becoming someone voters remember

None of these are required. All of them separate finalists from also-rans.

  • Long-form policy writing
  • Live AMA on Matrix or Spaces
  • Participation in candidate debates
  • Named endorsements from citizens
  • Track record of prior contribution

§ 3 — Get on the chain first

If you haven't used the blockchain yet, start here

The current Liberland blockchain is built on Substrate — the same framework Polkadot uses — and accepts standard Polkadot-ecosystem wallets. You need one before you can do anything else. The full onboarding flow is documented at docs.liberland.org; this is the short version.

1.

Confirm your status on the main website

Sign in at liberland.org. Confirm that your citizenship is active and that your Merit balance is at least 5,000. If anything is off, contact the Citizenship Office before going any further — fix on the website first, then on the chain.

2.

Install a wallet

Pick one and save the 12-word mnemonic seed phrase on paper. Anything you write it on must not be lost and must not be found by anyone else. Do not take a photo of it.

SubWallet
Mobile · Browser · Recommended

The wallet the docs are written around. Mobile app works well on iOS and Android, and there's a browser extension for desktop.

Talisman
Browser extension · Desktop

Polished extension for desktop browsers. Strong UX for managing multiple Substrate accounts at once.

Polkadot.js
Browser extension · Technical

The reference extension. Less polished but maximally compatible — works with everything in the Substrate world.

Ledger
Hardware · Polkadot app

If you hold significant LLD/LLM, use hardware. Install the Polkadot app on your Ledger to sign Liberland transactions.

3.

Visit blockchain.liberland.org and claim

Open the dApp at blockchain.liberland.org in the browser where your wallet is installed (or scan-pair from the mobile wallet). Follow the on-screen flow to claim your citizenship and your Merits on-chain. Merits typically arrive in about one business day after the claim is processed.

4.

Set your identity

On the dApp, open your profile and click Update identity. This is where you set your display name, legal name, and candidacy website — the three fields that appear next to your name on every ballot. Each field has a 32-character limit. For long URLs, use a shortener or ask a web admin for help.

The seed phrase is the only key

If you lose your 12-word phrase, no one in Liberland — not the Citizenship Office, not the validators, not the Provisional Government — can recover your account. If someone else gets it, they have your political identity and your Merits. Write it down on paper. Keep it somewhere safe. Tell no one.

About the candidate website field

The website field on the candidate list points to wherever a voter goes to learn about you. You have two choices:

  • Your own URL. Anything you control — a personal site, a Forum thread, a LinkedIn page, a Substack post. Keep the URL short or shorten it; the 32-character limit is strict.
  • An official liberland.org candidate page. The site can host candidate pages at liberland.org/elections. Send a document with the content you'd like on your page to a web admin and they will set it up. For an example of the format, see past candidate sites under that URL.

Once your page exists, set its URL in your on-chain profile via the Update identity action.

§ 5 — Strongly encouraged

What separates real candidates from names on a list

Liberland citizens are spread across dozens of countries. They will not meet you in person. Most of them will form their opinion of you from whatever you have published, whatever you say in the Forum, and whatever short video you make. If those things don't exist, you are competing only on name recognition — and most candidates don't have any.

The spot

A short campaign video

Optional in the strictest sense — the chain doesn't check for it — but in practice this is the single most valuable piece of campaigning a candidate can produce. A one-to-three minute video where you, on camera, explain who you are, why you're running, and what you would actually do in Congress.

It does not need production value. It needs to be you, speaking directly, saying something real. A clear, honest two-minute take filmed on a phone outperforms a polished but generic clip every time. Citizens are voting on whether they trust you to make decisions on their behalf for the next term. A face and a voice make that judgement possible.

Embed it on your candidate page, link it in your Forum introduction, share it in Liberland Chat. Keep it pinned for the duration of the campaign.

Record and publish a short campaign video (1–3 min)

§ 6 — Optional differentiators

What separates finalists from also-rans

None of these are required. Many successful candidates have run without them. But they consistently appear in the campaigns of citizens who finish at the top of the rankings. Pick the ones that fit your style and your time.

Publish a longer policy piece
A 1,500–3,000 word essay on one issue you'd actually work on in Congress. Written documents get re-read; videos get watched once. Both have their place.
Optional
Host an AMA in Liberland Chat
Pick a time, announce it 48 hours in advance on the Forum, show up, answer everything. An hour of honest questions is worth a week of broadcast posts.
Optional
Participate in candidate debates
When community-organised debates happen — and they often do — show up. Declining sends a stronger signal than any of your campaign material.
Optional
Collect named endorsements
Citizens, current and former Congress members, validators, ministry staff. Endorsements only count when they're public, named, and tied to a real on-chain identity.
Optional
Document your prior contribution
If you've already done work for Liberland — code, writing, organising, validator operation, judicial scaffolding, public events — link it. Track record beats promises.
Optional
Use social media if it's already part of your life
X/Twitter and Telegram reach LLD holders and the wider crypto-adjacent audience. Useful if you have a presence there. Not worth building from scratch for one campaign.
Optional

§ 7 — The voting method

How voters will actually decide

The current Liberland blockchain uses Weighted Phragmén — the same algorithm Polkadot uses to elect its on-chain bodies. It's a single algorithm with one job: convert a set of voters (each with some weight in Merits) and their candidate preferences into a Congress, while wasting as few votes as possible.

What a voter actually does

A voter selects every candidate they would be happy to see in Congress. There is no fixed limit on how many. They can rank them in order of preference if they like — but the ranking only matters for selecting the Speaker of Congress later, not for electing members.

The algorithm then redistributes weight intelligently. If a voter chose three candidates and the first two are already comfortably elected by other voters' weight, the algorithm shifts that voter's weight toward their third choice, where it might actually make a difference. This is the "vote isn't wasted" property and it changes how candidates should think about their base.

What this means for your strategy

↑ What works under Phragmén

  • Being the candidate a broad set of voters are comfortable adding to their selection — not just their first choice.
  • Substantive positions on real issues. Most voters select multiple candidates; you want to be on the list, not necessarily at the top.
  • Reaching beyond your existing supporters. Even a marginal addition to a voter's selection can swing the algorithm in your favour.
  • Visibility late in the cycle. Because votes can be updated at any time, undecideds often pick their last selections in the final days.

↓ What backfires

  • Polarising positions. There is no formal downvote, but citizens who actively dislike you simply leave you off their selection — and that's most of what matters.
  • Attacking other candidates by name. It tends to push their supporters to drop you from their list rather than convert them.
  • Vague platitudes. Voters who have ten candidates to evaluate skim hard. Specificity is what catches them.
  • Disappearing after applying. Silence between submission and election day reads as "doesn't actually want this."

Delegation: don't be surprised by it

Voters who don't want to evaluate every candidate themselves can delegate their voting power to a sitting member of Congress. That delegate's selections then apply with the delegator's Merit weight added. This means a respected Congress member can move significant weight by including you in their selection — and excluding you moves it the other way. Cultivate relationships with sitting Congress members early, not the week before the cycle ends.

Speaker of Congress: where preference ordering matters

Once Congress is elected, the preference order in each voter's selection is used to pick the Speaker. If you have ambitions beyond a seat — toward chairing the chamber — you also need voters to put you near the top of their list, not just on it.

§ 8 — The cycle

"Election moments", not election days

Liberland Congressional elections conclude every 90 days, but they are not single-day events. The current docs use a specific phrase for what actually happens: election moments. Votes do not reset between cycles — unless a citizen actively changes their selection, their previous vote carries forward into the next cycle automatically.

This changes how a candidate should think about timing:

  • You don't get a 14-day window of attention. You get whatever attention you earn from citizens whenever they happen to log in and reconsider their selections.
  • Applying just before a cycle ends is too late. Most voters set their selection weeks earlier and never change it. Apply early in the cycle and start being visible immediately.
  • Once you're in the candidate list, you stay in it until you withdraw or the cycle resolves. Don't wait for "campaign season" — Liberland politics doesn't really have one.
  • Delegation persists too. A delegator who picked a sitting member months ago is still delegating now. If your relationship with that member changes, the weight behind your name changes too — silently, on its own.

The practical implication

Treat campaigning as a continuous activity, not a sprint. Candidates who appear three weeks before the cycle ends, push hard, then disappear, do worse than candidates who are quietly visible for the full 90 days. The chain rewards persistence.

§ 9 — Conduct & integrity

What earns trust — and what loses it permanently

Everything you publish during a campaign sits on the public Forum, on Matrix, and often on-chain. The Liberland community is small enough that bad-faith conduct is remembered across election cycles. The few candidates whose reputations have been damaged in past cycles got there by the same handful of mistakes.

The things candidates lose campaigns over

  • Hidden financial conflicts. If you have a commercial interest that touches Liberland policy, disclose it in your candidate page. Citizens forgive disclosed conflicts; they do not forgive discovered ones.
  • Vote-buying. Soliciting LLM-weighted votes in exchange for any benefit, on-chain or off, is a constitutional violation. Don't.
  • Sockpuppet endorsements. Endorsements from anonymous or newly-created accounts are easy to spot and uniformly counterproductive.
  • Personal attacks on other candidates. In Phragmén, the cost of being attacked is that the attacker's supporters drop the target from their list — but the attacker often loses voters faster. It rarely converts anyone.
  • Going dark after applying. The cycle is 90 days. Disappearing for 80 of them and then asking for votes is a strong signal of how you'll behave in office.

The standard you're held to

The Liberland Constitution describes the country as a meritocratic, rule-of-law polity built on voluntary cooperation. Citizens evaluating candidates apply the same standard. The candidates who finish well are usually the ones who treat the campaign like a job interview for a position they take seriously — not a marketing exercise, not a personality contest.

§ 10 — After the cycle

Win or lose, what comes next

If you're elected to Congress

You take your seat immediately when the cycle resolves. From that point you have the powers and duties described in the Constitution and the Citizenship Law: proposing and passing legislation (subject to public referenda), passing budgets (subject to Senate veto), and the rest of the Congressional remit. The five-hours-a-week minimum is not a guideline; it is the floor. Citizens watch attendance and participation in the next cycle's selection.

If you're not elected this cycle

Your application remains active. Your Merits are unaffected. Your political rights are unchanged. And — because votes persist across cycles — the citizens who selected you this cycle will still be selecting you in the next one, unless they actively change their minds. The natural strategy is to keep doing what made them pick you in the first place: stay visible, stay substantive, stay in the conversation.

If you want to withdraw

You can remove your candidacy at any time from the Congress tab. Your Merits remain yours. Withdrawal is reversible — you can reapply in a later cycle without penalty.

A note on losing well

The best thing a defeated candidate can do is publish a short post — on the Forum, on Matrix — acknowledging the result, thanking voters, and saying what they intend to keep working on. Because votes persist across cycles, this is also the most direct way to keep the support you already have for the next round.

§ Summary

The shortest version of this guide

  1. Confirm eligibility. Full citizen, 5,000+ Merits, not on welfare.
  2. Onboard. Install a wallet (SubWallet, Talisman, Polkadot.js, or Ledger), claim your citizenship and Merits at blockchain.liberland.org.
  3. Apply. Submit via the Congress tab. Set your display name, legal name, and website on your profile (32-character limit each).
  4. Publish your campaign video. One to three minutes. You, on camera, telling the truth.
  5. Build a candidate page. Your own URL or an official one at liberland.org/elections. Link it in your on-chain profile.
  6. Be present where citizens are. Forum threads, Matrix chat, any community AMAs or debates.
  7. Run cleanly. Disclose conflicts, avoid personal attacks, no sockpuppets.
  8. Stay visible across the full 90 days. Votes persist; attention is continuous.
  9. Whatever the result, close it well. Thank voters, name what you'll keep working on.

The constitutional truth

Liberland's election system is built on the Constitution. The current blockchain and its election rules are how the Constitution is implemented today — they have evolved before and will evolve again. The Constitution is the only document that is the truth. If you intend to stand for Congress, you will be expected to know it. Read it before you apply: docs.liberland.org.
