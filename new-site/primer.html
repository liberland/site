A Primer for Citizens

Stand for
Congress.

A practical guide for citizens who want to run — what the system requires, what the community expects, and how to make a campaign people actually remember.

The constitutional truth

Twenty-one seats. Stake-weighted voting. The Constitution is the law; everything else is execution.

The on-chain mechanism

Register, lock a deposit, get voted on with the D21 method. Everything verifiable on Ethereum L2.

The human work

Be known. Say what you believe. Show up where citizens gather. The blockchain doesn't campaign for you.

§ Introduction

Why this guide exists

Liberland's Congress is elected by its citizens, on-chain, with stake-weighted votes. The blockchain handles the counting. Everything that gets you elected happens before that — and most of it is on you.

This primer walks through everything a serious Congressional candidate should do. Some items are mandatory: without them, your candidacy literally cannot be registered on-chain. Some are strongly encouraged: candidates who skip them tend to be invisible on election day. Some are optional but differentiating: they're how a candidate becomes recognisable, trusted, and remembered.

Liberland is small. The full electorate is in the low thousands. That means two things: a single well-made video can reach a meaningful share of voters, and a single bad-faith move can follow you for years. Treat both ends seriously.

§ 1 — Prerequisites

Before anything else, the eligibility check

The on-chain system enforces five conditions before it will accept your candidacy registration. These are evaluated by CandidateEligibilityPolicy at the moment you call registerCandidate(). None of them are negotiable, and none can be waived.

RequirementWhere it's checkedWhat "fail" looks like
You are a verified citizen of Liberland IdentityRegistry E-residents and applicants cannot run. KYC must be complete.
You are an adult by the registry definition IdentityRegistry Minors cannot run; this is a Constitution-level requirement.
You have the minimum active stake (5,000+ LLM in good standing) StakeRegistry Liquid LLM doesn't count. It must be politically pooled (staked).
No active unstaking cooldown on your stake StakeRegistry If you've started unstaking and the cooldown hasn't elapsed, you're locked out for this cycle.
No final suspension against you IdentityRegistry Citizens under final judicial suspension cannot stand.

Read this before you stake

If your LLM is not currently staked (politically pooled), staking it now means it counts. But starting to unstake before or during a candidacy window will disqualify you from this election. If in doubt, leave your stake alone for the full cycle.

§ 2 — The work, at a glance

Three tiers of candidate work

Not everything in this primer is equally important. Treat the three tiers as priorities, not a menu. The mandatory items must be done. The encouraged items are where serious candidates actually win or lose. The optional items separate good campaigns from memorable ones.

▼ Mandatory

The on-chain registration

Without these, your candidacy literally does not exist in the system.

  • Verified blockchain identity
  • Minimum 5,000 LLM staked
  • Candidacy registered on-chain
  • Candidacy deposit locked
  • All within the 14-day window
▼ Strongly encouraged

Becoming someone voters know

Skipping these means most citizens have no idea who you are on election day.

  • A short campaign video (1–3 min)
  • A written candidate statement
  • An introduction post on the Forum
  • Presence in Liberland Chat (Matrix)
  • Clear position on 2–3 issues
▼ Optional

Becoming someone voters remember

None of these are required. All of them separate finalists from also-rans.

  • Long-form policy writing
  • Live AMA on Matrix or Spaces
  • Participation in candidate debates
  • Endorsements from named citizens
  • Track record of prior contribution

§ 3 — Mandatory

What you must do

These are the items the smart contracts and the election law require. They are binary — either done or not — and there is no campaign without them.

Your blockchain identity is set up and verified
Citizen ID linked to your EVM wallet address in IdentityRegistry. If you've onboarded through the standard Liberland flow (email + password, or WalletConnect), this is already done.
Required
At least 5,000 LLM is politically pooled (staked)
Liquid balance does not count toward eligibility. Stake must be active — no unstaking cooldown in progress.
Required
You have a clean status in the system
No final suspension against you. If you've had any judicial proceedings, check your status in your DigiID dashboard before registering.
Required
Your candidacy is registered on-chain during the 14-day window
Submit through the Congress Election app. The transaction calls registerCandidate() and locks your deposit. Registration outside this window is simply not accepted.
Required
Your candidacy deposit is locked
An anti-spam deposit is locked when you register. It is returned to all candidates after the election, regardless of whether you won. The amount is set by current governance and visible in the registration interface before you confirm.
Required

Tie-break: earlier registration wins

If two candidates finish with identical D21 scores, the one whose registerCandidate() transaction was confirmed earlier on-chain ranks higher. This is rare but real. If you intend to stand, there is no reason to wait until the last day.

Withdrawing your candidacy

You can withdraw any time during the candidacy phase by calling withdrawCandidate(). Your deposit is returned. Once voting starts, withdrawal is closed — your name remains on the ballot and votes against you still count. Decide early.

§ 4 — Strongly encouraged

What separates real candidates from names on a list

Liberland citizens are spread across dozens of countries. They will not meet you in person. Most of them will form their opinion of you from whatever you have published, whatever you say in the Forum, and whatever short video you make. If those things don't exist, you are competing only on name recognition — and most candidates don't have any.

The spot

A short campaign video

Optional in the strictest sense — no smart contract checks for it — but in practice this is the single most valuable piece of campaigning a candidate can produce. A one-to-three minute video where you, on camera, explain who you are, why you're running, and what you would do in Congress.

It does not need production value. It needs to be you, speaking directly, saying something real. A clear, honest two-minute take filmed on a phone outperforms a polished but generic clip every time. Citizens are voting on whether they trust you to make decisions on their behalf for the next term. A face and a voice make that judgement possible.

Post it on the Forum, link it in your candidacy statement, share it in Liberland Chat. Keep it pinned for the duration of the campaign.

Record and publish a short campaign video (1–3 min)
Who you are, why you're running, what you'd actually do. Speaking directly to the camera. Phone-quality is fine.
Encouraged
Write a candidate statement
300–800 words. Your background, your priorities, your stance on the 2–3 issues you actually care about. Treat it as the document people read after they watch the video.
Encouraged
Introduce yourself on the Liberland Forum
A dedicated thread at forum.liberland.org. Pin your video, paste your statement, link your wallet address so citizens can verify your on-chain registration. Reply to comments.
Encouraged
Be present in Liberland Chat (Matrix)
The active citizen channels are where day-to-day political life happens. Lurking is fine; being completely absent is not. Answer questions when asked.
Encouraged
State your position on 2–3 substantive issues
Pick the issues you actually have something to say about. Generic libertarian talking points are background noise — citizens will reward specifics: what you'd vote for, what you'd vote against, what you'd propose.
Encouraged
Make your contact details findable
Forum profile, Matrix handle, optional email. Citizens who want to ask you something before voting need a way to do it.
Encouraged

§ 5 — Optional differentiators

What separates finalists from also-rans

These are not required. Many successful candidates have run without them. But they consistently appear in the campaigns of citizens who finish in the top of the rankings. Pick the ones that fit your style and your time.

Publish a longer policy piece
A 1,500–3,000 word essay on one issue you'd actually work on in Congress. Written documents get re-read; videos get watched once. Both have their place.
Optional
Host an AMA in Liberland Chat
Pick a time, announce it 48 hours in advance on the Forum, show up, answer everything. An hour of honest questions is worth a week of broadcast posts.
Optional
Participate in candidate debates
If community-organised debates happen — and they often do — show up. Declining sends a stronger signal than any of your campaign material.
Optional
Collect named endorsements
Citizens, current and former Congress members, validators, registrars. Endorsements only count when they're public, named, and tied to a real on-chain identity.
Optional
Document your prior contribution
If you've already done work for Liberland — code, writing, organising, validator operation, judicial scaffolding — link it. Track record beats promises.
Optional
Run a personal site or campaign page
Optional, but useful if you want one canonical link to share. Keep it minimal — citizens will follow it back to the Forum anyway.
Optional
Use social media if it's already part of your life
X/Twitter and Telegram reach LLD holders and the wider crypto-adjacent audience. Useful if you have a presence there. Not worth building from scratch for one campaign.
Optional

§ 6 — The voting method

How voters will actually decide

Liberland uses the Janeček D21 method, with each citizen's vote weighted by their staked LLM. Understanding this changes how you campaign — because it changes what voters can do with you on the ballot.

Each voter has:

  • Up to four positive votes ("thumbs up") to give to different candidates.
  • Up to one negative vote ("thumbs down") to give to a single candidate.

Each vote is weighted by the voter's politically pooled LLM at the start of the voting period. Your final score is the sum of positive vote-weights minus the sum of negative vote-weights. Scores can be negative. The 21 highest scores fill Congress; the next 10 become runners-up.

What this means for your strategy

↑ What works under D21

  • Being a candidate that broadly acceptable citizens can give one of their four positive votes to.
  • Clear positions stated calmly — citizens reward specificity.
  • Reaching beyond your existing supporters. The marginal positive vote often comes from someone who agrees with you on one issue out of three.
  • Visible decency. The downvote in D21 is rare but devastating — it costs the full weight of a large stakeholder, not just one vote.

↓ What backfires

  • Polarising positions designed to excite a base. Under D21, the citizens you repel matter as much as the ones you energise.
  • Attacking other candidates by name. This tends to invite reciprocal negative votes and rarely converts anyone.
  • Vague platitudes. With only four positive votes to allocate, voters prefer candidates with at least one concrete position.
  • Disappearing after registration. Silence between candidacy registration and election day is read as "doesn't actually want this."

A worked example

Five candidates, three voters (Alice with 10,000 LLM staked, Bob with 50,000, Carol with 5,000). Alice gives +1 each to A, B, C and a −1 to E. Bob gives +1 each to A, B, D and a −1 to C. Carol gives +1 each to B, C, D, E and a −1 to A.

Candidate B finishes first with 65,000 (everyone supported, no one opposed). Candidates A and D tie at 55,000 — A wins the tie on earlier registration. Candidate C drops to −35,000 because Bob's single downvote at 50,000 LLM weight overwhelmed two smaller positive votes. One large-stakeholder downvote can sink a candidate two small upvotes had supported. The system is meritocratic in the constitutional sense: stake matters.

§ 7 — The election cycle

What happens, when, and what you do

Phase 1 — Candidacy Registration 14 days
Get on the ballot, get yourself known
Eligible citizens call registerCandidate(); the deposit locks. Minimum 22 candidates must register for the election to proceed (one more than the number of seats). If fewer than 22 register, the phase extends by 7 days; if still under 22, all registered candidates win by default. What you do: register early in the window, publish your video and statement on the Forum, introduce yourself on Matrix.
Phase 2 — Voting 14 days
Citizens cast and change votes on-chain
Each vote is a transaction that records voter, candidate, and direction (+/−). Votes are publicly verifiable. Vote weight is snapshotted at the start of this phase. What you do: stay visible, answer questions, do an AMA if you can, participate in any community-organised debates. Stop posting promotional material in the last 48 hours — it reads as desperation.
Phase 3 — Tallying Automatic
On-chain finalisation
Scores compute automatically at end of voting. Top 21 → Congress; next 10 → runners-up. Results are immutable. A 14-day challenge window follows, during which the Supreme Court can review procedural issues. What you do: nothing on-chain; thank supporters and people who debated honestly with you.
Phase 4 — Seating After challenge period
The new Congress takes office
After the 14-day challenge period clears, the new Congress is seated. The previous Congress remains in office until that moment, so there is no governance gap. All candidate deposits are returned regardless of outcome.

By-elections and vacancies

If a Congress member resigns, is dismissed, or is removed by Public Veto, the highest-ranked runner-up takes the seat automatically. Only if no runners-up remain does a by-election trigger, using the same D21 method but with a shortened 7-day candidacy and 7-day voting period. Runners-up: stay engaged, your seat may still come.

§ 8 — Conduct & integrity

What earns trust — and what loses it permanently

Everything you publish during a campaign sits on the public Forum, on Matrix, and often on-chain. The Liberland community is small enough that bad-faith conduct is remembered across election cycles. The few candidates whose reputations have been damaged in past cycles got there by the same handful of mistakes.

The things candidates lose campaigns over

  • Hidden financial conflicts. If you have a commercial interest that touches Liberland policy, disclose it in your candidate statement. Citizens forgive disclosed conflicts; they do not forgive discovered ones.
  • Coordinated vote-buying. Soliciting LLM-weighted votes in exchange for any benefit is treated as a constitutional violation. Don't.
  • Sockpuppet endorsements. Endorsements from anonymous or newly-created accounts are easy to spot and uniformly counterproductive.
  • Personal attacks on other candidates. A downvote in D21 is procedural, not editorial — attacking another candidate by name in your campaign material tends to mobilise their supporters' downvotes against you.
  • Going dark after registration. Citizens read silence as either bad faith or lack of commitment. Both are reasons not to vote for you.

The standard you're held to

The Liberland Constitution describes the country as a meritocratic, rule-of-law polity built on voluntary cooperation. Citizens evaluating candidates apply the same standard. The candidates who finish well are usually the ones who treat the campaign like a job interview for a position they take seriously — not a marketing exercise, not a personality contest.

§ 9 — After the vote

Win or lose, what comes next

If you're elected to Congress

You will be seated after the 14-day challenge window. From that point you have the powers and duties described in the Constitution and the Citizenship Law: voting on referenda submitted by Congress, exercising the limited delay power for formal defects, and the rest of the Congressional remit. The on-chain CongressRegistry reflects your status; services that gate on Congress membership recognise you automatically.

If you're a runner-up

The next 10 candidates by score become runners-up. If a seat opens during the term, the highest-ranked available runner-up fills it without an election. This is not a consolation prize — runners-up have filled seats in real cycles. Stay engaged. Keep contributing.

If you finish further down — or with a negative score

A negative score is not a disqualification. It simply means more weighted opposition than support in this cycle. Your deposit is returned, your citizenship is unaffected, your political rights are unchanged, and nothing stops you running again in the next cycle. Many citizens who serve in Congress lost their first race. The community remembers candidates who run a clean, substantive campaign even when they don't win.

A note on losing well

The best thing a defeated candidate can do is publish a short post — on the Forum, on Matrix — acknowledging the result, thanking voters, and saying what they intend to keep working on. It costs nothing and almost always pays back the next time you stand.

§ Summary

The shortest version of this guide

  1. Confirm eligibility. Citizen, adult, 5,000+ LLM staked, no cooldown, no suspension.
  2. Register on-chain during the 14-day candidacy window. Deposit locks; tie-break favours earlier registration.
  3. Publish your campaign video. One to three minutes. You, on camera, telling the truth.
  4. Publish your candidate statement and pin it on the Forum. Stake out 2–3 real positions.
  5. Be present where citizens are. Forum threads, Matrix chat, any community AMAs or debates.
  6. Run cleanly. Disclose conflicts, avoid personal attacks, no sockpuppets.
  7. Stay visible through the voting period. Answer questions; don't disappear.
  8. Whatever the result, close it well. Thank voters, name what you'll keep working on.

The constitutional truth

Liberland's election system is built on the Constitution. The Referendum Law and the on-chain mechanics are how the Constitution is implemented — they can be changed by lawful process. The Constitution is the only document that is the truth. If you intend to stand for Congress, you will be expected to know it. Read it before you register: docs.liberland.org.
